The Thesis

Content vs. Capital

Most of what the internet calls a creator business is not a business. According to Net Influencer, 71% of full time creators earn under $30,000 a year. It is a job that rents its customers from a platform, and it stops paying the moment the camera turns off. Call this Tier 3, the platform tier, where the money and the reach both belong to someone else.

One rung up is the knowledge tier, Tier 2, where a creator sells a course, a cohort, or a community. The ceiling is higher and the margins are better. The economics still depend on the creator continuing to show up, so the income still slows the moment attention does.

The top tier is capital. Software, directories, tools, the kinds of assets that run when nobody is watching. The margin is the point. AI collapsed the cost of building these assets. According to GitHub's 2024 developer survey, 92% of US developers now use AI coding tools daily, which means the bottleneck is no longer access to engineering but the clarity to know what to build. That is what this site documents.

Three Investor Tests

What separates income from capital

01

The Recurring Test

Does money show up whether you posted this week or not. If the answer is no, you do not have a business. You have a shift, and the clock starts over every Monday.

02

The Absence Test

If you disappeared for a month, does the operation survive. Real businesses have systems, customers with problems, and software that keeps running. A brand that collapses in your absence is a performance, not a company.

03

The Ownership Test

If the platform deleted your account tonight, can you still reach your audience tomorrow. The answer is either a list you own, a product people log in to, or silence. Most creators find out the answer on the worst day of their year.
